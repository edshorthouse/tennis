The Ultimate World Champion

A lineal title in the spirit of boxing. One player holds it, and it only changes hands on the court, when the champion is beaten.

The Ultimate World Champion is an imaginary lineal title for men's tennis. It begins with the winner of the very first Open Era match, Owen Davidson on 22 April 1968, and passes to a new player only when the reigning champion loses. Every result is drawn from Jeff Sackmann's ATP data, limited to the events that have run consistently since 1968: Grand Slams, Masters, ATP 500 and 250 tournaments, and the Tour Finals.

There is no vacancy rule. A champion who retires without ever losing keeps the title, which is why it currently rests with a player from an earlier era.

The current reign Pete Sampras won the title at the 2002 US Open, beating Andre Agassi on 26 August 2002. That turned out to be the last match he ever played. He retired without losing it, so his reign is counted up to that date rather than rolling on through the rest of the data. The title has been dormant ever since.

Data sources and chain integrity

A lineal title is only as trustworthy as the single match that hands it over, so the chain is built to be audited. It runs on a frozen Jeff Sackmann base (1968 to 2024), extended with a live TennisMyLife tail (2025 to 2026), and every title-changing match is cross-checked against independent compilations.

Flagged baton hops

The title changes worth a second look: ambiguous same-day ordering, a non-completed result, or a match an independent source does not carry. Each one links out to the primary records to settle it.
